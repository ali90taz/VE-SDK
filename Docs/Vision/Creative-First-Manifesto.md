<h2 align="center">
Creative-First Manifesto
</br>
<br>
</h2>

<h4 align="left">
<b>VitaEngine</b> is being envisioned as a platform centered around the 
experience of the <b>creative end user</b>.
<br>
<br>
This does not mean rejecting technical depth, low-level knowledge, or the 
remarkable work already done by the <b>PS Vita homebrew scene</b>.
<br>
<br>
It simply means that, in its long-term direction, VitaEngine intends to place 
special value on <b>accessibility, workflow clarity, iteration speed, and a more 
welcoming path to creation</b>.
<br>
<br>
This document exists as a <b>long-term philosophical reference</b> for that 
intention.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>Important note</h3>

<h4 align="left">
The ideas described here represent the <b>long-term vision</b> behind VitaEngine.
<br>
<br>
They should not be read as a criticism of the PS Vita homebrew scene, nor as a 
claim that the project already fully delivers the experience described here.
<br>
<br>
VitaEngine is still an evolving project, and like any ambitious independent 
effort, it may continue to change, mature, or even remain unfinished.
<br>
<br>
What this document aims to preserve is not certainty, but <b>direction</b>.
<br>
<br>
It exists to make one thing clear:
<br>
<br>
<b>if VitaEngine continues to evolve, it should remain aligned with a 
creative-first philosophy.</b>
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>Why this philosophy matters</h3>

<h4 align="left">
The <b>PS Vita</b> remains one of the most inspiring handheld systems ever made.
<br>
<br>
Its homebrew scene has already shown that the hardware still has creative life, 
and that there are talented people willing to push it further.
<br>
<br>
At the same time, for many people, the path into creating for the Vita can still 
feel difficult to approach.
<br>
<br>
That is not a judgment against the existing ecosystem.
<br>
<br>
It is simply an observation that the current path may still ask a lot from a new 
creator before their first meaningful result:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li>Setup steps</li>
  <li>Toolchain familiarity</li>
  <li>Build system friction</li>
  <li>Low-level concepts early in the journey</li>
</ul>
<br>
For some people, that challenge is part of the appeal.
<br>
For others, it may become a barrier before the creative process even begins.
<br>
<br>
VitaEngine is being imagined with that second group in mind as well.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>Respect for the scene comes first</h3>

<h4 align="left">
VitaEngine would not even be imaginable without the extraordinary work already 
done by the PS Vita homebrew scene.
<br>
<br>
That foundation deserves respect.
<br>
<br>
The intention of VitaEngine is <b>not</b> to dismiss, replace, or diminish that 
work.
<br>
<br>
If anything, VitaEngine exists because the scene proved that the platform still 
matters, still inspires, and is still worth building for.
<br>
<br>
This project is better understood as an attempt to build <b>on top of that 
foundation</b>, with a different emphasis:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li>More integrated workflows</li>
  <li>More approachable entry points</li>
  <li>More direct iteration on real hardware</li>
  <li>A stronger focus on the creator-facing experience</li>
</ul>
<br>
That is not a statement that previous approaches were wrong.
<br>
It is simply a statement that a different kind of experience may also be worth 
pursuing.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>A platform centered on creative momentum</h3>

<h4 align="left">
One of the core ideas behind VitaEngine is that <b>creative momentum matters</b>.
<br>
<br>
In many projects, especially creative ones, the first successful result is 
important.
<br>
<br>
The sooner a creator can go from:
<br>
<br>
<i>“I want to make something for PS Vita”</i>
<br>
to
<br>
<i>“I am seeing something real running on a PS Vita”</i>
<br>
<br>
the more likely they are to continue, learn, refine, and stay engaged.
<br>
<br>
This does not mean the work becomes easy.
<br>
It does not mean the hardware becomes simple.
<br>
It does not mean technical depth disappears.
<br>
<br>
It only means that the first part of the journey may be designed to feel more 
encouraging.
<br>
<br>
That is one of the long-term goals VitaEngine hopes to support.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>Technical depth still matters</h3>

<h4 align="left">
VitaEngine is not based on the idea that technical depth is unimportant.
<br>
<br>
In fact, the platform itself may depend heavily on:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li>Native code</li>
  <li>Platform-specific behavior</li>
  <li>Performance-sensitive decisions</li>
  <li>Careful engine architecture</li>
  <li>Real constraints imposed by the PS Vita hardware</li>
</ul>
<br>
All of that remains important.
<br>
<br>
What VitaEngine hopes to explore is a different question:
<br>
<br>
<b>how much of that depth needs to be the creator’s first experience?</b>
<br>
<br>
A platform can be technically serious internally while still trying to offer a 
more approachable user-facing workflow.
<br>
<br>
That is not a rejection of low-level work.
<br>
It is simply a different emphasis.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>The value of humane abstraction</h3>

<h4 align="left">
Every serious software platform relies on some form of abstraction.
<br>
<br>
VitaEngine intends to treat abstraction not as an escape from reality, but as a 
tool for improving the creator’s experience where it makes sense.
<br>
<br>
That may include:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li>Reducing repetitive setup work</li>
  <li>Providing a more cohesive workflow</li>
  <li>Allowing higher-level project creation in <b>Lua</b></li>
  <li>Improving iteration between desktop and real hardware</li>
  <li>Making experimentation feel less fragile</li>
</ul>
<br>
The goal is not to hide the platform in a dishonest way.
<br>
The goal is to reduce unnecessary distance between an idea and its first real 
execution.
<br>
<br>
If complexity is necessary, it should be respected.
<br>
If complexity can be softened responsibly, it may be worth doing.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>Why user experience is treated as a serious concern</h3>

<h4 align="left">
In creative tools, user experience is not only about comfort.
<br>
<br>
It is also about:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li>Momentum</li>
  <li>Confidence</li>
  <li>Willingness to keep going</li>
  <li>The ability to iterate without exhaustion</li>
</ul>
<br>
A creator who spends too much energy fighting setup, fragmented workflows, or 
unfriendly tooling may never reach the stage where their ideas begin to grow.
<br>
<br>
Because of that, VitaEngine aims to treat the user-facing experience as a 
<b>serious design concern</b>, not a cosmetic one.
<br>
<br>
That includes long-term interest in:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li>Clearer project workflows</li>
  <li>More integrated tooling</li>
  <li>Faster iteration cycles</li>
  <li>Real hardware testing as part of the loop</li>
  <li>A more coherent platform identity</li>
</ul>
<br>
The intention is not only to make PS Vita development possible, but to make it 
feel more natural to continue.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>Real hardware as part of the loop</h3>

<h4 align="left">
One of the most exciting parts of developing for a console like the PS Vita is 
the hardware itself.
<br>
<br>
Whenever possible, the long-term goal is for real hardware to feel like part of 
the creative loop, not just the final destination.
<br>
<br>
That is part of why the VitaEngine ecosystem may include components such as the 
<b>VitaEngine Companion</b>, intended to help bridge the gap between desktop-side 
creation and real-device testing as part of a more integrated workflow :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}
<br>
<br>
This is not meant to remove the value of traditional workflows.
<br>
It is simply an attempt to explore a more direct and motivating one.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>Who this philosophy hopes to include</h3>

<h4 align="left">
VitaEngine hopes to be useful not only to technically advanced users, but also 
to people who may say:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li>“I want to make something for the Vita, but I need a friendlier way in.”</li>
  <li>“I want to prototype faster before I dive deeper.”</li>
  <li>“I want to learn by building, not only by preparing the environment.”</li>
  <li>“I want the hardware to feel inspiring, not distant.”</li>
</ul>
<br>
These creators are not less serious.
<br>
They may simply be earlier in the journey, or working from a different place.
<br>
<br>
A healthy ecosystem can benefit from both:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li>People who understand the deepest technical layers</li>
  <li>People who need a more approachable path before reaching them</li>
</ul>
<br>
VitaEngine is intended to care deeply about that second path, without denying the 
importance of the first.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>What this project is trying to protect</h3>

<h4 align="left">
Over time, the implementation may change.
Internal architecture may evolve.
Features may be added, removed or rethought.
<br>
<br>
But the long-term intention is to protect a few principles:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li><b>The creator’s momentum matters</b></li>
  <li><b>The first meaningful result should arrive sooner, not later</b></li>
  <li><b>User experience should be treated as part of the platform, not decoration</b></li>
  <li><b>Real hardware should feel connected to the workflow</b></li>
  <li><b>Accessibility and seriousness can coexist</b></li>
  <li><b>A project can respect technical depth without making it the only entry point</b></li>
</ul>
<br>
These principles are not meant to reject the past.
<br>
They are meant to guide a possible future direction.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>What this manifesto is <i>not</i> saying</h3>

<h4 align="left">
To avoid misunderstanding, this document is <b>not</b> claiming that:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li>The current PS Vita homebrew scene is doing anything wrong</li>
  <li>Low-level workflows are invalid or undesirable</li>
  <li>Every creator should avoid technical depth</li>
  <li>VitaEngine is already the superior approach</li>
  <li>This project is guaranteed to be completed</li>
</ul>
<br>
Instead, this manifesto simply expresses a personal and project-level intention:
<br>
<br>
<b>if VitaEngine continues to evolve, it should try to become a more welcoming, 
more integrated, and more creator-friendly way to build for the PS Vita.</b>
<br>
<br>
That is the aspiration.
<br>
Not a verdict.
<br>
<br>
</h4>

---

<h3>Final statement</h3>

<h4 align="left">
VitaEngine is being built around a simple belief:
<br>
<br>
<b>the PS Vita deserves tools that help more people create for it.</b>
<br>
<br>
Not because the existing scene failed.
<br>
Not because technical depth lacks value.
<br>
Not because complexity is always wrong.
<br>
<br>
But because there may still be room for a platform that places stronger emphasis 
on:
<br>
<br>
<ul>
  <li>Creative momentum</li>
  <li>Workflow clarity</li>
  <li>Friendly abstraction</li>
  <li>Real hardware iteration</li>
  <li>A more cohesive user-facing experience</li>
</ul>
<br>
If VitaEngine succeeds, it should succeed not by denying what came before, but by 
building respectfully on top of it with a different priority:
<br>
<br>
<b>to make creation on the PS Vita feel more approachable, more direct, and more 
inspiring for the people who want to build something real.</b>
<br>
<br>
</h4>
